Treaty 7 History

Events and decisions that led to the signing in 1877.

Background

During the 1800s, Canada expanded west. Settlers moved into prairie regions. The government wanted agreements with Indigenous nations for land use.

At the same time, major changes affected Plains First Nations. The buffalo population declined. Many communities faced food shortages and economic pressure.

Government officials proposed treaties across western Canada. These agreements aimed to organize land use and reduce conflict.

Events Before the Treaty

  • Expansion of Canadian settlement into prairie regions.

  • Government efforts to negotiate treaties with Indigenous nations.

  • Decline of buffalo populations on the plains.

  • Increased contact between Indigenous nations and government officials.

  • Negotiations for agreements about land and resources.

The Signing in 1877

Treaty 7 negotiations took place at Blackfoot Crossing in southern Alberta. Leaders from several First Nations met with Canadian representatives.

Discussions lasted several days. Leaders asked questions about land, hunting rights, and government support.

The final agreement included reserve land, yearly payments, and support for farming.

After the Treaty

  • Reserve lands were created for the nations involved.

  • The government began providing annual payments.

  • Schools and farming supplies were promised.

  • Treaty rights became part of Canadian law.

Timeline

  • Early 1800s. Plains nations rely on buffalo for food and trade.

  • 1870s. Buffalo populations decline across the plains.

  • 1877. Treaty 7 negotiations begin at Blackfoot Crossing.

  • September 1877. Treaty signed by leaders and officials.

  • Late 1800s. Reserve lands established.
